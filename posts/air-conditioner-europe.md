No, My Air Conditioner Is Not Killing Europe

Some people are now floating the idea that American air conditioning is somehow responsible for Europeans roasting during a heat wave.

And I have to say, what a beautiful little pile of sanctimonious nonsense.

Not because climate change is fake. It isn't.

Not because the United States has no emissions problem. It does.

Not because Americans are flawless stewards of energy. We are not.

But because this argument takes a complicated global industrial problem and turns it into a stupid cartoon where some guy in Florida setting his thermostat to seventy-two is apparently firing a climate laser across the Atlantic and melting Paris.

It is the kind of argument that sounds profound only if nobody is allowed to do math.

So let's do some.

According to EDGAR's twenty twenty-five greenhouse gas report, total greenhouse gas emissions in twenty twenty-four, excluding land use and forestry, were about five point nine one billion metric tons of carbon dioxide equivalent for the United States.

For France and Monaco, the number was about three hundred seventy-eight million.

For the European Union, it was about three point one six billion.

And for China, it was about fifteen point five four billion.

EDGAR also lists China, the United States, India, the European Union, Russia, and Indonesia as the world's largest emitters in twenty twenty-four.

So yes, at first glance, America emits a lot.

Nobody serious should deny that.

The United States is a massive industrial economy with over three hundred million people, huge agriculture, huge logistics, huge manufacturing, huge military infrastructure, and enormous transportation distances.

It is not a cute little museum country where half the population can take a train ten minutes to buy a baguette.

The U.S. is enormous.

That matters.

Now let's talk about land area.

Using World Bank land-area figures, the United States is about nine point one five million square kilometers.

France is about five hundred thirty-nine thousand square kilometers.

The European Union is about four million square kilometers.

And China is about nine point three nine million square kilometers.

So what happens when we flatten the comparison by land area?

The United States emits roughly six hundred forty-six tons of greenhouse gases per square kilometer.

France and Monaco emit roughly seven hundred two tons per square kilometer.

The European Union emits roughly seven hundred ninety-two tons per square kilometer.

And China emits roughly one thousand six hundred fifty-five tons per square kilometer.

Well, would you look at that.

When you compare emissions to the physical size of the country, France is not some pristine little climate monastery being murdered by Bubba's window unit.

France actually emits more greenhouse gas per square kilometer than the United States.

The European Union does too.

And China absolutely blows everyone off the chart.

That does not mean America is clean.

It means the lazy moral framing is crap.

The United States emits about fifteen point six times what France emits, but the United States is about seventeen times the land area.

That is a pretty important detail to leave out when you are wagging your finger across the ocean.

And then there is China.

China emits about two point six times as much as the United States while being roughly comparable in land area.

So if the goal is to discuss global emissions honestly, China is not a footnote.

China is the elephant sitting in the room, eating the curtains, while everyone argues about whether Americans are allowed to sleep without sweating through their sheets.

But of course, saying "China emits fifteen and a half billion tons" is not as emotionally satisfying as saying, "Americans like air conditioning, therefore Europe is dying."

And that is the problem with this entire line of argument.

It is not analysis.

It is theater.

It is climate blame packaged for social media.

It is a moral performance for people who want a villain more than they want a solution.

And the villain, conveniently, is always the same.

Americans.

Americans drive too much.

Americans cool their houses too much.

Americans eat wrong.

Americans live wrong.

Americans are apparently the reason your train was late, your wine was warm, your radiator doesn't work, and your government spent decades pretending summers would remain polite forever.

Sorry, but no.

Europe has its own choices.

France has its own policies.

Paris has its own infrastructure.

If a wealthy modern city is getting hit with dangerous heat, and large numbers of homes, hospitals, schools, and public spaces are not adequately cooled, that is not just an American emissions problem.

That is also an adaptation problem.

That is a planning problem.

That is a "we treated air conditioning like vulgar American barbarism until the weather stopped cooperating" problem.

You do not get to sneer at air conditioning for decades, refuse to build around the reality of hotter summers, and then turn around and blame the people who installed the machines that help keep their elderly from dying indoors.

That is not moral superiority.

That is bad planning with better table manners.

And yes, before someone gets their organic linen pants twisted into a decorative knot, per-capita emissions are a real counterpoint.

On a per-person basis, the United States is high.

Higher than France.

Higher than the European Union.

That matters.

It is a fair criticism.

Americans consume a lot of energy. We live spread out. We drive a lot. We cool large homes. And we have generally behaved like energy is cheap because, for a long time, it was.

Fine.

Put that in the argument.

But do not pretend that one metric is the entire truth while all the other metrics are rude interruptions.

Total emissions matter.

Per-capita emissions matter.

Industrial output matters.

Energy mix matters.

Imports matter.

Geography matters.

Density matters.

Weather matters.

Adaptation matters.

Nuclear power matters.

And whether your country built houses to retain heat in winter but not survive brutal summer nights also matters.

This is not a children's book.

There is not one bad dragon named American Air Conditioning.

And honestly, air conditioning is not just some frivolous luxury.

It is life-safety infrastructure.

Heat kills people.

Especially the elderly, the sick, the poor, and people trapped in dense urban heat islands.

A fan and a damp towel are not a climate policy.

They are what you do when the adults failed to prepare.

The answer to hotter summers is not to shame people for cooling buildings.

The answer is to produce cleaner energy, build better grids, use efficient heat pumps, improve insulation, harden cities against heat, plant shade, reduce waste, and stop pretending that suffering through dangerous temperatures is a virtue.

Because that is the really deranged part of this whole debate.

Some people have managed to turn not dying of heat stroke into a character flaw.

Apparently being comfortable in your own home is decadence now.

Apparently protecting grandma from a hundred-degree apartment is colonial arrogance.

Apparently the moral high ground is sweating in a brick oven while congratulating yourself for not being American.

No thanks.

America has plenty to criticize.

We waste energy.

We build stupidly.

We drive too much.

We overcool buildings.

We have houses the size of minor European villages.

There is a serious version of this conversation where the United States takes responsibility for its emissions and invests harder in cleaner power, better homes, better transportation, and more efficient cooling.

But "your air conditioning is killing Europeans" is not the serious version.

It is a bumper sticker for smug people.

It is the climate debate reduced to finger-pointing and vibes.

It is what happens when people would rather blame an American thermostat than admit that their own governments, cities, and cultural assumptions may not be ready for the world they now live in.

So yes, climate change is real.

Yes, the United States is a major emitter.

Yes, Americans should be more efficient.

Yes, there is work to do.

But no, my air conditioner is not personally assassinating Europeans.

And if your climate argument requires pretending it is, your argument is not brave or sophisticated.

It is just dumb with a passport.
